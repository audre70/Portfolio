TaskBuddy | Organization & task management for college students

To-do lists, schedules, assignments, and classes all in one app. A productivity tool built with students in mind that prioritizes structure and motivation.

Timeline

3 weeks
(Oct-Nov 2023)

Role

UX Designer (solo project)

Disciplines

UX Research
UX/UI Design
Interaction Design

Tools

Figma
Adobe Illustrator

CHALLENGE

In a fast-paced college environment, students frequently face difficulty with organization and task management given their many assignments and commitments. For this project, I sought to build a UI solution that addresses their needs through user research and testing.

As a foundation, I focused on delivering a polished minimum viable product (MVP) that included the ability to add a task, complete a task, and categorize tasks by class.

DESIGN APPROACH

To guide this process, I used the research-based Double Diamond Method of Design to guarantee the ideal user experience. This approach embraces iteration and discovery while presenting a clear path to the final delivery of the product.

PHASE 1: DISCOVERY

This phase focused on understanding the details of the problem at hand. For TaskBuddy, I saw an opportunity to better understand the pain points and preferences of my user base to contextualize the product before diving into the problem. Methods like mind mapping with peers and user discovery were used to achieve this.

🤔 How do users currently manage tasks?

In user discovery interviews, I aimed to familiarize myself students’ existing habits with organizing tasks and assignments. I asked questions such as: How do you organize your tasks? What motivates you to do your work? How close to deadlines do you get when completing tasks? Do you use any apps to organize your work?

Key insights from interviews with 6 college students revealed:

Students tend to prioritize + complete assignments based on how close due dates are

Calendar visualization is important to users, with Google Calendar being a particularly popular tool

In apps, students value categorization and color coding of tasks

The vast majority of students tend to procrastinate, but reminders are useful in helping them complete tasks

PHASE 2: DEFINING THE PROBLEM

LO-FI WIREFRAMES

Based on discoveries about the user group, a lo-fi wireframe attempt was crafted to address my findings through several features.

To-do list

Upon opening the app, users immediately are met with a to-do list of their tasks, ordered by approaching due date.

Navbar

Opted for this rather than a collapsible menu to maximize discoverability of different tools. Nav items include home, calendar, classes, settings, and “add a task.”

Adding a task

With adding a task being the central focus of the app, it was included as a button in the nav menu to be accessible from any page in the app.

Color Coding/Labeling

Every task has the ability to be color coded and labeled by class.

Ideating and getting feedback helped me hone in on the central issues students were facing: procrastination due to forgetting assignments until right before they were due; effort in setting reminders for tasks; and difficulty in visualizing their workload.

😵‍💫

STUDENTS ARE OVERWHELMED BY THEIR WORKLOAD AND STRUGGLE TO STUDY PROACTIVELY

🫤

OUR OPPORTUNITY: TaskBuddy can help streamline individuals’ intuitive study habits by offering different ways to visualize upcoming tasks and adding important details to tasks like dates, descriptions, colors, and notifications.

USER FLOW: Thinking About How Users Might Navigate Through the App

However, initial wireframes revealed that there needed to be a clearer intention behind the connections leading to different pages. Drawing several user flow charts helped me better empathize with users by thinking about possible paths they would take through the interface. I considered the main objective of the app—adding, organizing, and completing tasks—but also individual preference for task viewing formats.

Defining the flow made it possible to:

  • Find new connections on different pages where features (e.g. adding a task) may need to be accessed
  • Reduce or eliminate unnecessary components (excess number of edit buttons on different pages)

PHASE #3: DEVELOPING & BRAINSTORMING THE SOLUTION

We encountered several problems with the first wireframe️ ⚠️

“Add task” looked too similar to a nav link

Too many unnecessary buttons

So in the paper prototype, the following changes were made 🔨

Enlarged + distinguished “add task” button

Removed Classes page (but later re-added)

Fleshed out add and edit task modals

PAPER PROTOTYPES

USABILITY TESTING / MEDIUM-FIDELITY PROTOTYPES

I also conducted testing with medium fidelity prototypes to further refine features and ensure that I was meeting my goals for users' needs and expectations.

Users enjoyed the versatility of viewing tasks in checklist or calendar format, but few understood tasks were clickable on the checklist to see details (compared to the calendar). Users also inquired why information like time and category weren’t visible in the home hub.

Modified nav icons were universally understood among users.

Testing showed that task modals were overall effective in terms of understandability. To better signal that the tab was collapsible, I added a drag handle in this iteration stage.

Some users noted accessibility concerns for the colored text. There were also issues with buttons for deleting tasks or marking them complete not immediately appearing to be interactive. I ensured that these issues were solved in the final iteration.

Like in the paper prototype, the Classes page was omitted in this iteration to keep the central focus on the MVP. Instead, I opted to have “add/edit category” in a subpage in the settings.

However, I later re-added the nav item as I reconsidered the merits of accessible class information for students: info like class times, professor names, and external syllabi links.

PHASE #4: DELIVERY & FINAL PRODUCT

In follow-ups with previous usability test interviewees, users reported overwhelmingly positive feedback on the adjustments made to the issues that we faced. This final product is a result of several rounds of prototyping with user testing at each stage, with data informing every design decision that was made. Below I demonstrate the key features that were developed as part of this project.

To-do list and calendar supports users in visualizing workload

The to-do list offers users a way to complete tasks—sorted by importance/deadline—with ease by simply checking a box. The clickable area indicates that users can expand more details about the task, and color and time labels on both pages display useful information without having to enter a separate subpage. A horizontal scroll calendar allows you to reference future or past tasks quickly, and the month dropdown can even let the user skip far ahead.

A comprehensive page of courses, color coded

TaskBuddy helps students by not only facilitating completion of assignments and tasks, but also by keeping semester courses, clubs, and other commitments accessible and organized. In this one page, users can quickly reference their schedule to know when to head to their class or add a new category for a club they just joined. Clicking on a class reveals a subpage where you can edit the class info and color, add links like a syllabus, and view tasks that belong to that category.

Adding a task is made easy on any page because of its placement on the nav.

Made a mistake? No problem! Tasks can be easily edited or deleted anytime.

Usability testing showed that users consistently wanted the ability to complete tasks on all pages.

TaskBuddy makes completing tasks possible whether you’re in the list view, calendar, etc. You can check a box or mark completion in task view.

Offers motivation and encouragement for users

TaskBuddy shows messages of encouragement when users finish their tasks for the day, or when they don’t have any work, it reminds them to take care of themselves. Positive reinforcement and satisfaction is important when you’re working hard!

OTHER SCREENS

REFLECTION

This was my first project using Figma, and there was definitely a huge learning curve since I was learning software tools as I was designing. I can now say I’m much more confident using the software, practicing atomic design, and prototyping and testing in it.

I referenced a lot of UI components used by Apple and Google that users are accustomed to in order to communicate what I wanted in buttons, text fields, etc. However, this experience has shown me to I have a lot to learn about about design systems and inspired me to look into industry standards.

👀🌟

NEXT TIME

In future projects, I would make sure to make more digital copies of my different iterations—especially medium-fidelity wireframes in the usability testing phase of the product—to record my iterative design process that I put a lot of research into.

I would also look more into other similar apps and perform competitive analyses, because I retrospectively have learned that seeing other designs trying to accomplish similar goals can teach us a lot!
